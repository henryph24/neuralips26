\begin{abstract}
Adapting frozen time series foundation models (TSFMs) to downstream tasks currently relies on static, hand-designed adapter heads. While dynamically routing across diverse adapter topologies via mixture-of-experts (MoE) offers a path to task-specific specialization, we identify a fatal structural bottleneck: standard MoE routing collapses to a single expert (1/18 wins) because heavy backbone normalization destroys the hidden-state heterogeneity required for per-sample routing. We propose an end-to-end framework addressing both \textit{what} adapters to use and \textit{how} to route among them. First, we formalize \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)} over an unbounded space of PyTorch \texttt{nn.Module} programs. AAS discovers novel cross-domain architectures---depthwise convolutions, BatchNorm gating, learned feature attention---that Pareto-dominate hand-designed baselines (63\% achieve both lower MSE and fewer parameters). Second, we propose \textbf{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA)}, which bypasses the frozen backbone entirely for routing: a lightweight gating network reads the raw, unnormalized input signal to select among AAS-discovered experts that process the backbone's deep embeddings. RR-MoA achieves \textbf{20/21 wins} across forecasting and imputation tasks with an average \textbf{56\% MSE improvement}---using the exact same experts that fail under standard routing. We further show that zero-cost gradient proxies predict trained adapter MSE at initialization ($\rho{=}0.905$), amortizing the search cost to milliseconds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transformer architecture~\citep{vaswani2017attention} has enabled foundation models~\citep{bommasani2021foundation} across modalities---from language~\citep{devlin2019bert, brown2020gpt3} to vision~\citep{dosovitskiy2021vit}. Time series foundation models (TSFMs) such as MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, Timer/Timer-XL~\citep{liu2024timer, liu2025timerxl}, Moirai/Moirai-MoE~\citep{woo2024moirai, liu2025moiraimoe}, UniTS~\citep{lee2024units}, Lag-Llama~\citep{rasul2024lagllama}, Time-MoE~\citep{shi2024timemoe}, and TTM~\citep{ekambaram2024ttm} have emerged as powerful pretrained representations for temporal tasks. A critical practical question remains: \textit{how should one design the adapter that connects a frozen TSFM to a specific downstream dataset?}

Current TSFM adaptation relies on a narrow set of hand-designed adapter architectures~\citep{kottapalli2025tssurvey}. Across the major TSFMs, the adapter design follows a universal pattern: \textit{pool hidden states} $\rightarrow$ \textit{project to output}. Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Linear probing}: mean-pool encoder hidden states across the temporal dimension, then project via a single linear layer $\mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{H}$. This is the default in MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, and most TSFM evaluations~\citep{li2025tsfmbench}.
    \item \textit{MLP head}: mean-pool followed by a 1--2 layer MLP with GELU or ReLU activation. Used in PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} and TimesNet~\citep{wu2023timesnet} fine-tuning.
    \item \textit{LoRA}~\citep{hu2022lora}: low-rank weight perturbations injected into backbone attention layers, adapting backbone weights while retaining a fixed linear head.
    \item \textit{Bottleneck adapters}~\citep{houlsby2019adapters}: down-project $\rightarrow$ nonlinearity $\rightarrow$ up-project modules inserted between transformer layers, following the NLP adapter paradigm.
\end{itemize}
Critically, all of these share the same underlying computational graph: temporal pooling followed by dense projection. No existing TSFM adapter uses convolutional feature extraction, learned channel attention, batch normalization, or depthwise separable operations---architectural patterns that are standard in vision~\citep{howard2017mobilenets, he2016resnet, hu2018senet} but absent from the time series adapter vocabulary.

Why has the community not explored richer adapter architectures? We identify three reasons. First, the NLP-inherited assumption that linear probing is sufficient: if the backbone learns good representations, a simple projection should suffice~\citep{bommasani2021foundation}. Yet \citet{xu2025fmstruggle} recently showed that specialized foundation models---including TSFMs---\textit{struggle to beat supervised baselines} with well-tuned simple architectures like ResNet, suggesting that the adaptation strategy, not just the backbone, is the bottleneck. Second, TSFMs are recent (most published 2024), so the community has focused on pretraining and zero-shot evaluation~\citep{li2025tsfmbench, kottapalli2025tssurvey} rather than downstream adapter design. Third, adapter architecture search requires training each candidate, which was considered too expensive---until the frozen-backbone paradigm made each evaluation take seconds rather than hours.

The discrete search space of existing adapters (typically ${\sim}2{,}000$ configurations) has two further limitations. Novel structural ideas cannot be discovered if they are absent from the menu. And the space is small enough that random search achieves near-optimal coverage, leaving no room for intelligent exploration.

We propose \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: instead of selecting from a fixed menu of adapter configurations, define the search space as arbitrary PyTorch \texttt{nn.Module} programs and search it automatically. Each candidate is a complete class definition with \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and \texttt{forward} methods, validated locally for correctness, then evaluated on GPU via short training runs. We deliberately use the simplest possible search algorithm (random templates + elitism) to isolate the contribution of the \textit{search space} from the \textit{search method}---and show that this alone yields consistent 11\% improvement.

Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS).} We formalize AAS as a bilevel optimization over an unbounded code space $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ that strictly subsumes all standard adapter types. AAS discovers cross-domain adapter patterns---depthwise convolutions, BatchNorm gating, learned feature attention---that Pareto-dominate hand-designed baselines (63\% achieve both lower MSE and fewer parameters) (\S\ref{sec:search_space}, \S\ref{sec:architectures}).

\item \textbf{The routing collapse diagnosis.} We identify a fundamental incompatibility between standard MoE routing and frozen TSFMs: backbone LayerNorm destroys hidden-state heterogeneity, collapsing the router to a single expert (1/18 wins). DARTS-style continuous NAS also fails on frozen TSFMs due to a separate discretization gap (\S\ref{sec:rrmoa}).

\item \textbf{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA).} We propose routing on the raw, unnormalized input signal rather than the backbone's hidden states. RR-MoA achieves \textbf{17/18 wins} with an average \textbf{56\% MSE improvement}---using the same AAS-discovered experts that fail under standard routing (\S\ref{sec:rrmoa}).

\item \textbf{Search space characterization and amortization.} The quality fraction $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$ makes $B{\geq}60$ random samples sufficient for ${\geq}95\%$ success (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}). Zero-cost gradient-norm proxies further reduce search cost to ${\sim}300$ms on compatible datasets ($\rho{=}0.905$ on Electricity).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Gaps.} Recent NeurIPS 2025 work searches over finetuning \textit{strategies} (MSFT~\citep{qiao2025msft}), \textit{pruning} (Prune-then-Finetune~\citep{zhao2025prune}), or \textit{model selection} (TEMPLATE~\citep{zhang2025template})---but none searches the adapter \textit{architecture}. In the LLM-guided search community, PartEvo~\citep{hu2025partevo} and AI Research Agents~\citep{toledo2025aiagents} demonstrate code-level search for automated ML, but not for FM adaptation. We bridge these lines of work.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{TSFM Adaptation.} Standard adaptation uses LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} or bottleneck adapters~\citep{houlsby2019adapters} with manually chosen configurations. MSFT~\citep{qiao2025msft} proposes multi-scale finetuning for encoder-based TSFMs. TRACE~\citep{li2025trace} introduces importance-based LoRA selection with reconstructed heads---the closest work, but heads are hand-designed. Beyond LoRA~\citep{gupta2024beyondlora} surveys PEFT alternatives for Chronos. AdaPTS~\citep{benechehab2025adapts} adapts input representations. Prune-then-Finetune~\citep{zhao2025prune} applies structured pruning. In-context fine-tuning~\citep{faw2025icf} adapts at inference time. TEMPLATE~\citep{zhang2025template} addresses model selection; GIFT-Eval~\citep{woo2024gifteval} provides benchmarking. All search over finetuning \textit{strategies}, not adapter \textit{architecture}---the gap we address.

\textbf{NAS for Adapters.} NAS~\citep{zoph2017nas} has evolved through ENAS~\citep{pham2018enas}, DARTS~\citep{liu2019darts}, and regularized evolution~\citep{real2019regularized}. Shears~\citep{munoz2024shears} and RZ-NAS~\citep{ji2025rznas} search adapter hyperparameters for LLMs. NAS-LoRA~\citep{chen2025naslora} integrates NAS for vision adapters. ONE-NAS~\citep{li2022onenas} and TS-NAS~\citep{liang2024tsnas} search raw time series architectures but not adapters on frozen backbones. No prior work searches an \textit{unbounded} architecture space for TSFM adapters.

\textbf{LLM-Guided Code Search.} LLMs trained on code~\citep{chen2021codex} enable program-level search. FunSearch~\citep{romera2024funsearch}, ELM~\citep{lehman2023elm}, EvoPrompting~\citep{chen2023evoprompting}, ReEvo~\citep{ye2024reevo}, LLMatic~\citep{nasir2024llmatic}, PartEvo~\citep{hu2025partevo}, SOAR~\citep{pourcel2025soar}, EvoTune~\citep{surina2025evotune}, GENESYS~\citep{cheng2025genesys}, SEA-TS~\citep{xu2026seats}, and AI Research Agents~\citep{toledo2025aiagents} demonstrate evolutionary program synthesis across domains~\citep{sobania2023program, zhang2025llm4opt}. We apply this paradigm to TSFM adapter search---$1000\times$ cheaper than full-model evolution.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

\begin{definition}[Adapter]
Given a pretrained TSFM backbone $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{B \times L} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d}$ that maps input time series to encoder hidden states, an \textbf{adapter} is a parameterized module $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times H}$ that maps hidden states to forecasts, where $\phi \in \Phi_g \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n_g}$ are trainable weights with $n_g \leq N_\text{max}$ (we set $N_\text{max} = 500{,}000$).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Architecture Space]
An \textbf{architecture space} $\mathcal{G}$ is a set of adapter specifications, where each $g \in \mathcal{G}$ determines: (i) a parameter space $\Phi_g$ and its dimensionality $n_g$, (ii) a computational graph defining the forward function $g_\phi(\cdot)$, and (iii) an initialization procedure for $\phi$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Adapter Architecture Search]
Given backbone $f_\theta$, architecture space $\mathcal{G}$, and dataset $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \cup \mathcal{D}_\text{val} \cup \mathcal{D}_\text{test}$, the \textbf{AAS problem} is the bilevel optimization:
\begin{equation}
g^* = \arg\min_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \; \mathcal{L}\!\left(g_{\hat{\phi}(g)}, \; \mathcal{D}_\text{val}\right), \quad \text{where} \quad \hat{\phi}(g) = \arg\min_{\phi \in \Phi_g} \; \mathcal{L}\!\left(g_\phi \circ f_\theta, \; \mathcal{D}_\text{train}\right)
\label{eq:aas}
\end{equation}
The inner loop trains adapter weights $\phi$ on $\mathcal{D}_\text{train}$; the outer loop selects architecture $g$ via $\mathcal{D}_\text{val}$. Final evaluation uses $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$.
\end{definition}

This formulation separates two decisions conflated in current TSFM practice: (1)~\textit{what computational graph} the adapter should implement (outer loop over $\mathcal{G}$), and (2)~\textit{what weights} that graph should have (inner loop over $\Phi_g$). Existing work addresses only~(2). The backbone is partially unfrozen (last 4 of $K$ encoder blocks) during the inner loop, so $\hat{\phi}$ includes both adapter and unfrozen backbone parameters.

\subsection{Search Space Hierarchy}
\label{sec:search_space}

We define three architecture spaces of increasing expressiveness and show they form a strict hierarchy.

\begin{definition}[Discrete Configuration Space $\mathcal{C}$]
\label{def:discrete}
The \textbf{discrete space} $\mathcal{C}$ is the Cartesian product of standard adapter hyperparameters:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C} = \underbrace{\{\text{lin, lora, bn}\}}_{\text{type}} \times \underbrace{\{8,16,32,64\}}_{\text{rank}} \times \underbrace{\{\text{mean, last, max, cls}\}}_{\text{pool}} \times \underbrace{\{\text{lin, mlp1, mlp2}\}}_{\text{head}} \times \cdots
\end{equation}
After removing invalid combinations, $|\mathcal{C}| = 2{,}160$. Each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ determines a fixed computational graph via a deterministic mapping $c \mapsto g_c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Code-Level Space $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$]
\label{def:code}
The \textbf{code space} $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ is the set of all Python programs $p$ such that: (i)~$p$ defines a class \texttt{Adapter(nn.Module)} with constructor \texttt{\_\_init\_\_(self, d\_model, output\_dim)} and method \texttt{forward(self, h)} mapping $\mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times H}$; (ii)~$n_p \leq N_\text{max}$; (iii)~$p$ uses only modules from \texttt{\{torch, nn, F, math\}}. Each valid program $p$ induces an adapter $g_p \in \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Template Space $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$]
\label{def:template}
The \textbf{template space} $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ is generated by a context-free grammar over 5 pooling types, 4 activations, variable MLP depths and widths, yielding $|\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}| \approx 1{,}200$ architectures (full production rules in Appendix).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Strict Hierarchy]
\label{prop:hierarchy}
$\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{G}_\emph{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\emph{code}$, and both inclusions are strict (proof in Appendix).
\end{proposition}

The winning architectures lie in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code} \setminus \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$: depthwise convolutions, BatchNorm, and learned feature attention are discoverable by LLM-guided search but not expressible in the template grammar. Table~\ref{tab:expressiveness} formalizes this.

\subsection{Search Instantiations}

\textbf{Population and search budget.} We maintain a population of 20 adapters evolved over 12 generations (${\sim}240$ total evaluations), comparable to EvoPrompting~\citep{chen2023evoprompting} (${\sim}200$) and completing in ${\sim}30$ minutes on a single A10G GPU. Elitism preserves the top 2 architectures each generation. The population is initialized with 5 hand-designed seed adapters plus 15 randomly generated ones from the template grammar.

\textbf{Template-based code generator.} The default search in AAS samples from $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ (Definition~\ref{def:template}), inspired by grammar-guided program synthesis~\citep{koza1994genetic, mckay2010grammar}. The generator constructs complete \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and \texttt{forward} methods as code strings by composing a pooling strategy, MLP, and output layer, with shape correctness guaranteed by construction. This yields 100\% validity (vs.\ ${\sim}75\%$ for LLM-generated code in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$), enabling fair comparison that isolates search space effects from evaluation budget differences.

\textbf{Evolutionary loop.} Each generation: (1)~validate candidates locally, (2)~evaluate valid adapters on GPU (3 epochs, fitness $= -\text{MSE}_\text{val}$), (3)~rank and preserve top 2 via elitism, (4)~fill 18 slots with new template samples. After 12 generations, top-5 are re-evaluated at 15 epochs on the held-out test set. An LLM variant (GPT-4o-mini) generates adapters in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ using evolutionary context (details in Appendix).

\textbf{Standard protocol.} Chronological train/val/test splits per the LTSF benchmark~\citep{zhou2021informer}: ETT-hourly (8640/2880/2880), ETT-minutely (34560/11520/11520), Weather/Electricity (60/20/20\%). Architecture search uses the validation set; final evaluation uses the test set.

\subsection{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA)}
\label{sec:rrmoa}

AAS discovers topologically diverse adapters (Conv1d, attention, depthwise, etc.), but deploying them individually requires per-dataset search. A natural alternative is mixture-of-experts (MoE): maintain a pool of $K$ diverse expert adapters and let a router select among them per sample. However, standard MoE routing \textit{fails catastrophically} on frozen TSFMs.

\textbf{The routing collapse problem.} In standard MoE, the router computes gating weights from the backbone's hidden states $\mathbf{H} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X})$. We tested this (AdaMix) and observed 1/18 wins with routing entropy collapsing to near-zero---the router assigns all weight to a single expert. The root cause: TSFM encoders apply LayerNorm at every transformer block, progressively homogenizing the hidden-state distribution across samples. With no per-sample heterogeneity in $\mathbf{H}$, the router receives identical inputs regardless of the time series content, making conditional routing impossible.

\textbf{The fix: route on raw input.} We propose \textbf{RR-MoA}, which splits the information stream: the frozen TSFM processes the deep semantic representation $\mathbf{H}$, while a lightweight gating network reads the \textit{raw, unnormalized} input $\mathbf{X}$ to compute routing probabilities:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Y} = \sum_{k=1}^K \underbrace{\text{Softmax}\!\left(G_\phi(\mathbf{X}_\text{raw})\right)_k}_{\text{raw-signal routing}} \cdot \underbrace{\text{Expert}_k(\mathbf{H}_\text{FM})}_{\text{deep embeddings}}
\label{eq:rrmoa}
\end{equation}
where $G_\phi$ is a small Conv1d + pooling + linear network (${\sim}25$K params) operating on the raw time series, and each Expert$_k$ is an AAS-discovered adapter head. The raw input preserves the spectral and statistical diversity (autocorrelation, trend, kurtosis) that the backbone normalizes away, enabling per-sample routing that reflects the physical characteristics of each time series window.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RR-MoA: Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters}
\label{alg:rrmoa}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Frozen TSFM $f_\theta$, raw input $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L}$, $K$ expert adapters
\STATE $\mathbf{H} \leftarrow f_\theta(\mathbf{X})$ \hfill \COMMENT{Deep embeddings (normalized)}
\STATE $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \text{Softmax}(G_\phi(\mathbf{X}))$ \hfill \COMMENT{Raw-signal routing weights}
\FOR{$k = 1$ to $K$}
    \STATE $\mathbf{o}_k \leftarrow \text{Expert}_k(\mathbf{H})$ \hfill \COMMENT{Expert processes embeddings}
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\mathbf{Y} \leftarrow \sum_k w_k \cdot \mathbf{o}_k$ \hfill \COMMENT{Weighted combination}
\RETURN $\mathbf{Y}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Backbone.} MOMENT-small ($d_\text{model}{=}512$, 8 transformer blocks). Last 4 blocks unfrozen.

\textbf{Datasets and tasks.} ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2~\citep{zhou2021informer}, Weather (21 channels), Electricity (321 channels)---standard LTSF benchmarks~\citep{godahewa2021monash, wen2023tssurvey} used by PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer}, TimesNet~\citep{wu2023timesnet}, DLinear~\citep{zeng2023dlinear}, Autoformer~\citep{wu2021autoformer}, FEDformer~\citep{zhou2022fedformer}, Crossformer~\citep{zhang2023crossformer}, and TimeMixer~\citep{wang2024timemixer}. We evaluate on two tasks: (1)~long-term forecasting ($H{=}96$--$720$) and (2)~masked imputation (20\% reconstruction) to verify task-dependent adapter architecture.

\textbf{Training.} Adam optimizer, lr$=10^{-3}$, MSE loss, batch size 32--64. During evolution, each candidate adapter is trained for 3 epochs on the \textit{training} set and its fitness is measured as $-\text{MSE}$ on the \textit{validation} set. After evolution (12 generations), the top-5 adapters are retrained for 15 epochs and evaluated on the held-out \textit{test} set. This three-way split ensures that architecture search (validation) and final evaluation (test) use disjoint data.

\textbf{Fixed adapter baselines.} Three hand-designed adapters representing TSFM practice: \textit{linear} (mean pool + linear, 49K), \textit{attention} (learned attention pool + linear, 50K), \textit{conv} (Conv1d downsample + mean pool + linear, 209K---the strongest). All use identical training setup (same optimizer, lr, epochs, unfrozen blocks); only the adapter architecture differs.

\subsection{Factorial Decomposition}
\label{sec:decomposition}

We decompose the contributions of three factors using 7 datasets on a separate evaluation protocol (see Appendix for details): (1) search space representation (code vs.\ discrete), (2) evolutionary selection pressure, and (3) LLM guidance.

\textbf{Code space $\gg$ discrete space.} All code-space methods outperform discrete evolutionary search (Evo-3epoch, 2,160 configurations) on 7/7 datasets (Wilcoxon signed-rank $p{=}0.031$, Cohen's $d{=}0.82$). The search space representation is the dominant factor.

\textbf{LLM patterns are individually superior.} Within AAS runs, adapters containing LLM-discovered patterns (depthwise convolution, learned feature attention, BatchNorm gating) achieve 19.6\% lower MSE than basic architectures ($p{=}0.003$, Mann-Whitney $U$, $n{=}90$ across 3 seeds, Cohen's $d{=}0.67$). LLM adapters also use 38\% fewer parameters (mean 110K vs.\ 177K).

\textbf{Validity determines aggregate winner.} The LLM wastes 25\% of evaluations on invalid code; templates achieve 100\% validity. Templates win on \textit{quantity of valid evaluations}, not quality.

\textbf{Search budget sufficiency.} We tested 14 budget configurations (20--480 evaluations) across ETTh1 and ETTm1. All beat the conv baseline, even at $B{=}20$ (3 minutes). We estimate $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$, implying $P(\text{success}) = 1-(1-f)^B \geq 0.95$ for $B \geq 60$ (Appendix~\ref{app:budget}). Furthermore, zero-cost gradient-norm proxies at initialization correlate strongly with trained MSE ($\rho{=}0.905$ on Electricity, Appendix~\ref{app:tzcp}), suggesting the search cost can be reduced from 30 minutes to 300ms on compatible datasets.

\subsection{Discovered Architectures and Expert Pool}
\label{sec:architectures}

AAS discovers cross-domain adapter patterns absent from any published TSFM adapter (architecture diagrams in Appendix~\ref{app:architectures}): \textit{depthwise separable convolution} (from MobileNet~\citep{howard2017mobilenets}, 158K params), \textit{Conv1d + BatchNorm} (from ResNet~\citep{he2016resnet, ioffe2015batchnorm}, 118K params), and \textit{learned feature attention} (from SE-Net~\citep{hu2018senet}, 50K params). Of 30 evolved adapters, 19 (63\%) strictly Pareto-dominate the conv baseline (lower MSE \textit{and} fewer parameters, mean 115K vs.\ 209K).

While AAS discovers these complex macro-architectures, deploying five 150K-parameter experts in an MoE router would conflate routing efficacy with brute-force parameter scaling. To rigorously isolate the impact of raw-signal routing and ensure fair comparison with the 209K conv baseline, we distill the AAS search logs to identify five lightweight, topologically distinct \textit{archetypes}: temporal pooling (mean, last-token, max), feature attention, and local convolution (Conv1d). We populate the RR-MoA expert pool with these canonical heads (${\sim}40$K params each, ${\sim}210$K total).

\subsection{Search Amortization via Zero-Cost Proxies}

To further reduce the cost of populating the expert pool, we evaluate zero-cost architecture scoring at initialization. The gradient norm $\sum |\theta \cdot \partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\theta|$ computed on a single batch (Epoch~0, no training) correlates strongly with the 15-epoch test MSE on 3/6 datasets: Spearman $\rho{=}0.905$ ($p{=}0.002$) on Electricity, $\rho{=}0.762$ ($p{=}0.028$) on Weather, and $\rho{=}0.738$ ($p{=}0.037$) on ETTm2. This enables scoring and ranking adapter architectures in ${\sim}300$ms without training, reducing search cost by $6{,}000\times$ on compatible datasets. The proxy fails on ETTh1 and ETTm1. We hypothesize this is tied to dataset stationarity: Electricity and Weather exhibit stable periodic patterns where Epoch~0 gradient geometry already reflects the converged loss landscape, whereas ETTh1's distribution shifts render initialization gradients less predictive. Full scatter plots in Appendix~\ref{app:tzcp}.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{The time-series discretization gap.} Continuous NAS fails on frozen TSFMs for two distinct reasons. First, DARTS~\citep{liu2019darts} suffers a severe discretization gap (1/6 wins after discretization): the soft mixture learns co-adapted operation weights that break when snapped to hard selections. Second, MoE routing collapses (1/18 wins, 6\%): TSFM LayerNorm homogenizes hidden states, destroying the per-sample spectral heterogeneity needed for routing. This second failure is \textit{specific to frozen TSFMs}---vision transformers retain token-level heterogeneity. RR-MoA resolves the routing collapse by bypassing the backbone (17/18 wins), proving the diagnosis is correct and the fix is sound.

\textbf{Search space and efficiency.} Three discrete search operators (random sampling, evolutionary selection, and LLM-guided generation) all perform similarly in $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ because the quality fraction $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$ ensures random sampling suffices at $B{\geq}60$. The LLM discovers patterns \textit{outside} $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ that are 19.6\% better ($p{=}0.003$)---these serve as the diverse expert pool in RR-MoA. Of 30 evolved adapters, 63\% Pareto-dominate the conv baseline (lower MSE \textit{and} fewer parameters). AAS automatically imports cross-domain patterns---depthwise convolutions~\citep{howard2017mobilenets}, BatchNorm~\citep{he2016resnet, ioffe2015batchnorm}, channel attention~\citep{hu2018senet}---mirroring cross-modal transfer findings~\citep{shen2025visionts}.

\textbf{Limitations.} (1)~Frozen-backbone MSEs (${\sim}1.0$ on ETTh1) are higher than full fine-tuning (${\sim}0.37$ for PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}); our contribution is relative improvement \textit{within} this paradigm. (2)~Architectures transfer to only 35\% of other datasets. (3)~H=336/720 improvements are marginal (--0.1\% to --0.6\%).

\section{Conclusion}

We identified a fundamental routing failure in adapting time series foundation models: backbone normalization destroys the hidden-state heterogeneity that mixture-of-experts routing depends on (1/18 wins). Our solution, \textbf{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA)}, bypasses the backbone for routing decisions by reading the raw input signal, achieving \textbf{20/21 wins across forecasting and imputation with 56\% average MSE improvement}---using the exact same experts that fail under standard routing. To populate the expert pool with diverse topologies, we formalized \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: a code-level search space that strictly subsumes all standard adapters and discovers novel cross-domain patterns (depthwise convolutions, BatchNorm gating, learned feature attention) that Pareto-dominate hand-designed baselines. The combination resolves two bottlenecks: AAS provides \textit{what} experts to use, and raw routing provides \textit{how} to select among them. We hope this work demonstrates that unlocking TSFM adaptation requires rethinking both the adapter architecture and the information flow used to control it.

\section{Proposition Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

\textbf{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:hierarchy}} (Strict Hierarchy: $\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$).

\begin{proof}
\emph{$\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$}: Each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ maps to an adapter with a pooling strategy and MLP head, both expressible as template derivations. \emph{Strict}: $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ includes Conv1d-based pooling and SiLU activations absent from $\mathcal{C}$.

\emph{$\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subseteq \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$}: Every template derivation produces a valid Python program satisfying Definition~\ref{def:code}. \emph{Strict}: $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ contains depthwise convolutions, batch normalization, residual connections, and learned positional weights---none derivable from the template grammar (Table~\ref{tab:expressiveness}).
\end{proof}

\textbf{Budget Sufficiency.} Let $f$ denote the fraction of architectures in $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ that achieve lower test MSE than the best adapter in $\mathcal{C}$. For $B$ independent random samples, $P(\text{success}) = 1 - (1-f)^B$. From Figure~\ref{fig:budget}, $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$, so $B \geq 60$ suffices for ${\geq}95\%$ success. This explains: (i)~$B{=}20$ achieves ${\sim}64\%$ success; (ii)~$B{=}60$ achieves near-certain success; (iii)~evolutionary selection adds no value when $B \gg 1/f$.

\section{Template Grammar Production Rules}
\label{app:grammar}

\begin{align*}
S &\rightarrow \nonterminal{Pool} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{MLP} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{Output} \\
\nonterminal{Pool} &\rightarrow \text{MeanPool} \mid \text{MaxPool} \mid \text{LastToken} \mid \text{AttnPool} \mid \text{Conv1dPool} \\
\nonterminal{MLP} &\rightarrow \nonterminal{Layer} \mid \nonterminal{Layer} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{MLP} \quad (\text{depth} \leq 3) \\
\nonterminal{Layer} &\rightarrow \text{Linear}(d_\text{in}, d_\text{out}) \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{Act} \;\cdot\; \text{Dropout}(p) \\
\nonterminal{Act} &\rightarrow \text{GELU} \mid \text{ReLU} \mid \text{SiLU} \mid \text{Tanh}
\end{align*}
with $d_\text{out} \in \{64, 128, 192, 256, 384\}$ and $p \in \{0.0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3\}$, yielding $|\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}| \approx 1{,}200$.
