\begin{abstract}
Standard mixture-of-experts (MoE) routing fails on time series foundation models (TSFMs): when we plug diverse adapter experts into a frozen TSFM backbone, the router collapses to a single expert (1/18 wins) because heavy encoder normalization destroys the hidden-state heterogeneity that routing depends on. We propose \textbf{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA)}, which bypasses the backbone entirely for routing decisions---a lightweight gating network reads the \textit{raw, unnormalized} input signal to select among architecturally diverse experts that process the backbone's deep embeddings. RR-MoA achieves \textbf{17/18 wins} with an average \textbf{56\% MSE improvement} across 6 datasets and 3 seeds, using the same experts that fail under standard routing. To populate the expert pool with diverse adapter topologies, we formalize \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: searching an unbounded space of PyTorch \texttt{nn.Module} programs that strictly subsumes all standard adapter types. AAS discovers novel architectures---depthwise convolutions, batch normalization gating, learned feature attention---that individually outperform basic designs by 19.6\% ($p{=}0.003$) and Pareto-dominate hand-designed baselines (63\% achieve both lower MSE and fewer parameters). The combination of AAS-discovered experts with raw-signal routing resolves two bottlenecks simultaneously: architecture diversity (what experts to use) and routing fidelity (how to select among them).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transformer architecture~\citep{vaswani2017attention} has enabled foundation models~\citep{bommasani2021foundation} across modalities---from language~\citep{devlin2019bert, brown2020gpt3} to vision~\citep{dosovitskiy2021vit}. Time series foundation models (TSFMs) such as MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, Chronos~\citep{ansari2024chronos}, Timer/Timer-XL~\citep{liu2024timer, liu2025timerxl}, Moirai/Moirai-MoE~\citep{woo2024moirai, liu2025moiraimoe}, UniTS~\citep{lee2024units}, Lag-Llama~\citep{rasul2024lagllama}, Time-MoE~\citep{shi2024timemoe}, and TTM~\citep{ekambaram2024ttm} have emerged as powerful pretrained representations for temporal tasks. A critical practical question remains: \textit{how should one design the adapter that connects a frozen TSFM to a specific downstream dataset?}

Current TSFM adaptation relies on a narrow set of hand-designed adapter architectures~\citep{kottapalli2025tssurvey}. Across the major TSFMs, the adapter design follows a universal pattern: \textit{pool hidden states} $\rightarrow$ \textit{project to output}. Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Linear probing}: mean-pool encoder hidden states across the temporal dimension, then project via a single linear layer $\mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{H}$. This is the default in MOMENT~\citep{goswami2024moment}, TimesFM~\citep{das2024timesfm}, and most TSFM evaluations~\citep{li2025tsfmbench}.
    \item \textit{MLP head}: mean-pool followed by a 1--2 layer MLP with GELU or ReLU activation. Used in PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst} and TimesNet~\citep{wu2023timesnet} fine-tuning.
    \item \textit{LoRA}~\citep{hu2022lora}: low-rank weight perturbations injected into backbone attention layers, adapting backbone weights while retaining a fixed linear head.
    \item \textit{Bottleneck adapters}~\citep{houlsby2019adapters}: down-project $\rightarrow$ nonlinearity $\rightarrow$ up-project modules inserted between transformer layers, following the NLP adapter paradigm.
\end{itemize}
Critically, all of these share the same underlying computational graph: temporal pooling followed by dense projection. No existing TSFM adapter uses convolutional feature extraction, learned channel attention, batch normalization, or depthwise separable operations---architectural patterns that are standard in vision~\citep{howard2017mobilenets, he2016resnet, hu2018senet} but absent from the time series adapter vocabulary.

Why has the community not explored richer adapter architectures? We identify three reasons. First, the NLP-inherited assumption that linear probing is sufficient: if the backbone learns good representations, a simple projection should suffice~\citep{bommasani2021foundation}. Yet \citet{xu2025fmstruggle} recently showed that specialized foundation models---including TSFMs---\textit{struggle to beat supervised baselines} with well-tuned simple architectures like ResNet, suggesting that the adaptation strategy, not just the backbone, is the bottleneck. Second, TSFMs are recent (most published 2024), so the community has focused on pretraining and zero-shot evaluation~\citep{li2025tsfmbench, kottapalli2025tssurvey} rather than downstream adapter design. Third, adapter architecture search requires training each candidate, which was considered too expensive---until the frozen-backbone paradigm made each evaluation take seconds rather than hours.

The discrete search space of existing adapters (typically ${\sim}2{,}000$ configurations) has two further limitations. Novel structural ideas cannot be discovered if they are absent from the menu. And the space is small enough that random search achieves near-optimal coverage, leaving no room for intelligent exploration.

We propose \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: instead of selecting from a fixed menu of adapter configurations, define the search space as arbitrary PyTorch \texttt{nn.Module} programs and search it automatically. Each candidate is a complete class definition with \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and \texttt{forward} methods, validated locally for correctness, then evaluated on GPU via short training runs. We deliberately use the simplest possible search algorithm (random templates + elitism) to isolate the contribution of the \textit{search space} from the \textit{search method}---and show that this alone yields consistent 11\% improvement.

Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The routing collapse diagnosis.} We identify a fundamental incompatibility between standard MoE routing and frozen TSFMs: backbone LayerNorm destroys hidden-state heterogeneity, collapsing the router to a single expert (1/18 wins). This \textit{time-series discretization gap} also invalidates DARTS-style continuous NAS on frozen TSFMs (\S\ref{sec:rrmoa}).

\item \textbf{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA).} We propose routing on the raw, unnormalized input signal rather than the backbone's hidden states. RR-MoA achieves \textbf{17/18 wins} with an average \textbf{56\% MSE improvement}---using the same experts that fail under standard routing. The routing entropy remains high (1.4--1.6 / 1.61 max), confirming genuinely diverse, sample-dependent expert selection (\S\ref{sec:rrmoa}).

\item \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS).} We formalize AAS as a bilevel optimization over an unbounded code space $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ that strictly subsumes all standard adapter types. AAS discovers cross-domain adapter patterns---depthwise convolutions, BatchNorm gating, learned feature attention---that Pareto-dominate hand-designed baselines (63\% achieve both lower MSE and fewer parameters) and serve as the diverse expert pool for RR-MoA (\S\ref{sec:search_space}, \S\ref{sec:architectures}).

\item \textbf{Search space characterization.} We prove that the quality fraction $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$ of the template grammar outperforms standard adapters, making $B{\geq}60$ random samples sufficient for ${\geq}95\%$ success. This explains why random search suffices and evolutionary selection adds no value---the search space, not the algorithm, is the bottleneck (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Gaps in the current landscape.} Recent NeurIPS 2025 work reveals a maturing but incomplete picture of TSFM adaptation. MSFT~\citep{qiao2025msft} demonstrates that naive finetuning underutilizes TSFM capabilities, yet restricts its search to finetuning \textit{strategies} (multi-scale modeling) rather than adapter \textit{architectures}. Prune-then-Finetune~\citep{zhao2025prune} shows that TSFMs contain redundant substructures and benefit from pruning before adaptation, but does not explore what \textit{new} architectural components might replace the pruned ones. TEMPLATE~\citep{zhang2025template} addresses which \textit{pretrained model} to select from a pool, but assumes the adapter architecture is given. The NeurIPS 2025 Workshop on Time Series Foundation Models (``Have We Reached the BERT Moment?'') highlighted that lightweight supervised baselines still match or exceed TSFMs in many settings---suggesting that the adaptation strategy, not just the backbone, is the bottleneck. Meanwhile, in the LLM-guided search community, PartEvo~\citep{hu2025partevo} and the AI Research Agents study~\citep{toledo2025aiagents} (Spotlight) demonstrate that search strategy design is critical for automated ML, but neither applies code-level search to foundation model adaptation. We bridge these two lines of work: applying evolutionary code search to the underexplored problem of adapter architecture design for TSFMs.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{TSFM Adaptation.} Standard adaptation uses LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} or bottleneck adapters~\citep{houlsby2019adapters} with manually chosen configurations. A growing body of work searches over \textit{how} to finetune, but not \textit{what} adapter architecture to use. MSFT~\citep{qiao2025msft} proposes multi-scale finetuning strategies for encoder-based TSFMs (Moirai, MOMENT, UniTS). TRACE~\citep{li2025trace} introduces importance-based LoRA module selection and reconstructed prediction heads---the closest work to ours---but the head architectures are still hand-designed rather than searched. Beyond LoRA~\citep{gupta2024beyondlora} surveys PEFT alternatives (BitFit, VeRA, FourierFT) for Chronos, comparing methods that modify \textit{backbone weights} rather than the adapter module itself. AdaPTS~\citep{benechehab2025adapts} learns feature-space transformations \textit{before} the frozen FM, adapting the input representation rather than the output architecture. Prune-then-Finetune~\citep{zhao2025prune} applies structured pruning before adaptation. In-context fine-tuning~\citep{faw2025icf} adapts TSFMs at inference time. TEMPLATE~\citep{zhang2025template} addresses \textit{model selection} via transferability metrics. GIFT-Eval~\citep{woo2024gifteval} provides a comprehensive evaluation benchmark. All of these search over finetuning \textit{strategies}, input \textit{transformations}, or model \textit{selection}---none search the adapter \textit{architecture} itself. This is the gap we address.

\textbf{NAS for Adapters.} Neural architecture search~\citep{zoph2017nas} has evolved from RL-based methods to weight-sharing approaches like ENAS~\citep{pham2018enas} and differentiable methods like DARTS~\citep{liu2019darts}. Regularized evolution~\citep{real2019regularized} demonstrated that evolutionary NAS matches or exceeds RL-based search. Shears~\citep{munoz2024shears} searches discrete adapter hyperparameters for LLMs. RZ-NAS~\citep{ji2025rznas} uses LLM reflection with zero-cost proxies. In vision, NAS-LoRA~\citep{chen2025naslora} integrates NAS blocks between LoRA encoder and decoder for SAM adaptation---the closest methodological peer, but applied to vision segmentation rather than time series. ONE-NAS~\citep{li2022onenas} and related evolutionary TS-NAS work~\citep{liang2024tsnas} search raw architectures for time series but not adapters on frozen backbones. TSFM-Bench~\citep{li2025tsfmbench} provides comprehensive benchmarking of TSFMs across adaptation strategies but does not explore adapter architecture search. No prior work searches an \textit{unbounded} architecture space for time series foundation model adapters.

\textbf{LLM-Guided Code Search.} Large language models trained on code~\citep{chen2021codex} can generate syntactically valid programs, enabling a new paradigm of code-level search. FunSearch~\citep{romera2024funsearch} demonstrated LLM-guided evolutionary program search for mathematical discovery. Evolution through Large Models (ELM)~\citep{lehman2023elm} showed LLMs can serve as mutation operators for design. EvoPrompting~\citep{chen2023evoprompting} combines LLMs with evolutionary search for neural architecture search. ReEvo~\citep{ye2024reevo} introduces reflective evolution for combinatorial optimization heuristics. LLMatic~\citep{nasir2024llmatic} pairs LLMs with MAP-Elites quality-diversity search. PartEvo~\citep{hu2025partevo} introduces niching for LLM-based algorithm discovery (NeurIPS 2025). SOAR~\citep{pourcel2025soar} adds self-improvement via hindsight learning (ICML 2025). EvoTune~\citep{surina2025evotune} fine-tunes the LLM search operator via RL. GENESYS~\citep{cheng2025genesys} uses multi-agent LLMs to evolve entire LM architectures at 14M--350M scale. SEA-TS~\citep{xu2026seats} applies MCTS-based code generation to time series forecasting. The AI Research Agents study~\citep{toledo2025aiagents} (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight) formalizes search strategies for automated ML. Recent surveys~\citep{sobania2023program, zhang2025llm4opt} formalize the broader landscape of LLM-driven evolutionary program synthesis. Our work applies this paradigm to TSFM \textit{adapter} search---1000$\times$ cheaper than full-model evolution---with a systematic decomposition of what drives improvement.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

\begin{definition}[Adapter]
Given a pretrained TSFM backbone $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{B \times L} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d}$ that maps input time series to encoder hidden states, an \textbf{adapter} is a parameterized module $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times H}$ that maps hidden states to forecasts, where $\phi \in \Phi_g \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n_g}$ are trainable weights with $n_g \leq N_\text{max}$ (we set $N_\text{max} = 500{,}000$).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Architecture Space]
An \textbf{architecture space} $\mathcal{G}$ is a set of adapter specifications, where each $g \in \mathcal{G}$ determines: (i) a parameter space $\Phi_g$ and its dimensionality $n_g$, (ii) a computational graph defining the forward function $g_\phi(\cdot)$, and (iii) an initialization procedure for $\phi$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Adapter Architecture Search]
Given backbone $f_\theta$, architecture space $\mathcal{G}$, and dataset $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \cup \mathcal{D}_\text{val} \cup \mathcal{D}_\text{test}$, the \textbf{AAS problem} is the bilevel optimization:
\begin{equation}
g^* = \arg\min_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \; \mathcal{L}\!\left(g_{\hat{\phi}(g)}, \; \mathcal{D}_\text{val}\right), \quad \text{where} \quad \hat{\phi}(g) = \arg\min_{\phi \in \Phi_g} \; \mathcal{L}\!\left(g_\phi \circ f_\theta, \; \mathcal{D}_\text{train}\right)
\label{eq:aas}
\end{equation}
The inner loop trains adapter weights $\phi$ on $\mathcal{D}_\text{train}$; the outer loop selects architecture $g$ via $\mathcal{D}_\text{val}$. Final evaluation uses $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$.
\end{definition}

This formulation separates two decisions conflated in current TSFM practice: (1)~\textit{what computational graph} the adapter should implement (outer loop over $\mathcal{G}$), and (2)~\textit{what weights} that graph should have (inner loop over $\Phi_g$). Existing work addresses only~(2). The backbone is partially unfrozen (last 4 of $K$ encoder blocks) during the inner loop, so $\hat{\phi}$ includes both adapter and unfrozen backbone parameters.

\subsection{Search Space Hierarchy}
\label{sec:search_space}

We define three architecture spaces of increasing expressiveness and show they form a strict hierarchy.

\begin{definition}[Discrete Configuration Space $\mathcal{C}$]
\label{def:discrete}
The \textbf{discrete space} $\mathcal{C}$ is the Cartesian product of standard adapter hyperparameters:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C} = \underbrace{\{\text{linear, lora, bottleneck}\}}_{\text{adapter type}} \times \underbrace{\{8, 16, 32, 64\}}_{\text{rank}} \times \underbrace{\{\text{mean, last, max, cls\_mean\_max}\}}_{\text{pooling}} \times \underbrace{\{\text{linear, mlp1, mlp2}\}}_{\text{head}} \times \cdots
\end{equation}
After removing invalid combinations, $|\mathcal{C}| = 2{,}160$. Each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ determines a fixed computational graph via a deterministic mapping $c \mapsto g_c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Code-Level Space $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$]
\label{def:code}
The \textbf{code space} $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ is the set of all Python programs $p$ such that: (i)~$p$ defines a class \texttt{Adapter(nn.Module)} with constructor \texttt{\_\_init\_\_(self, d\_model, output\_dim)} and method \texttt{forward(self, h)} mapping $\mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times H}$; (ii)~$n_p \leq N_\text{max}$; (iii)~$p$ uses only modules from \texttt{\{torch, nn, F, math\}}. Each valid program $p$ induces an adapter $g_p \in \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Template Space $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$]
\label{def:template}
The \textbf{template space} $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ is generated by a context-free grammar $(\mathcal{V}, \Sigma, \mathcal{R}, S)$ with production rules:
\begin{align*}
S &\rightarrow \nonterminal{Pool} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{MLP} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{Output} \\
\nonterminal{Pool} &\rightarrow \text{MeanPool} \mid \text{MaxPool} \mid \text{LastToken} \mid \text{AttnPool} \mid \text{Conv1dPool} \\
\nonterminal{MLP} &\rightarrow \nonterminal{Layer} \mid \nonterminal{Layer} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{MLP} \quad (\text{depth} \leq 3) \\
\nonterminal{Layer} &\rightarrow \text{Linear}(d_\text{in}, d_\text{out}) \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{Act} \;\cdot\; \text{Dropout}(p) \\
\nonterminal{Act} &\rightarrow \text{GELU} \mid \text{ReLU} \mid \text{SiLU} \mid \text{Tanh}
\end{align*}
with $d_\text{out} \in \{64, 128, 192, 256, 384\}$ and $p \in \{0.0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3\}$. This yields $|\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}| \approx 1{,}200$ architectures.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Strict Hierarchy]
\label{prop:hierarchy}
$\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$, and both inclusions are strict.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\emph{$\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$}: Each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ maps to an adapter with a pooling strategy and MLP head, both expressible as template derivations. \emph{Strict}: $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ includes Conv1d-based pooling and SiLU activations absent from $\mathcal{C}$.

\emph{$\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subseteq \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$}: Every template derivation produces a valid Python program satisfying Definition~\ref{def:code}. \emph{Strict}: $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ contains architectures with depthwise convolutions, batch normalization, residual connections, and learned positional weights---none derivable from the template grammar. These are the architectures discovered by AAS (Table~\ref{tab:expressiveness}).
\end{proof}

The hierarchy is the key theoretical insight: the \textit{winning} architectures lie in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code} \setminus \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$---they were discovered by LLM-guided search in the unbounded code space, but even searching the smaller $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ already outperforms searching $\mathcal{C}$ ($p{=}0.031$).

\subsection{Search Instantiations}

\textbf{Population and search budget.} We maintain a population of 20 adapters evolved over 12 generations (${\sim}240$ total evaluations), comparable to EvoPrompting~\citep{chen2023evoprompting} (${\sim}200$) and completing in ${\sim}30$ minutes on a single A10G GPU. Elitism preserves the top 2 architectures each generation. The population is initialized with 5 hand-designed seed adapters plus 15 randomly generated ones from the template grammar.

\textbf{Template-based code generator.} The default search in AAS samples from $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ (Definition~\ref{def:template}), inspired by grammar-guided program synthesis~\citep{koza1994genetic, mckay2010grammar}. The generator constructs complete \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and \texttt{forward} methods as code strings by composing a pooling strategy, MLP, and output layer, with shape correctness guaranteed by construction. This yields 100\% validity (vs.\ ${\sim}75\%$ for LLM-generated code in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$), enabling fair comparison that isolates search space effects from evaluation budget differences.

\textbf{Evolutionary loop.} Each generation: (1)~validate candidates locally, (2)~evaluate valid adapters on GPU (3 epochs, fitness $= -\text{MSE}_\text{val}$), (3)~rank and preserve top 2 via elitism, (4)~fill 18 slots with new template samples. After 12 generations, top-5 are re-evaluated at 15 epochs on the held-out test set. An LLM variant (GPT-4o-mini) generates adapters in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ using evolutionary context (details in Appendix).

\textbf{Standard protocol.} Chronological train/val/test splits per the LTSF benchmark~\citep{zhou2021informer}: ETT-hourly (8640/2880/2880), ETT-minutely (34560/11520/11520), Weather/Electricity (60/20/20\%). Architecture search uses the validation set; final evaluation uses the test set.

\subsection{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA)}
\label{sec:rrmoa}

AAS discovers topologically diverse adapters (Conv1d, attention, depthwise, etc.), but deploying them individually requires per-dataset search. A natural alternative is mixture-of-experts (MoE): maintain a pool of $K$ diverse expert adapters and let a router select among them per sample. However, standard MoE routing \textit{fails catastrophically} on frozen TSFMs.

\textbf{The routing collapse problem.} In standard MoE, the router computes gating weights from the backbone's hidden states $\mathbf{H} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X})$. We tested this (AdaMix) and observed 1/18 wins with routing entropy collapsing to near-zero---the router assigns all weight to a single expert. The root cause: TSFM encoders apply LayerNorm at every transformer block, progressively homogenizing the hidden-state distribution across samples. With no per-sample heterogeneity in $\mathbf{H}$, the router receives identical inputs regardless of the time series content, making conditional routing impossible.

\textbf{The fix: route on raw input.} We propose \textbf{RR-MoA}, which splits the information stream: the frozen TSFM processes the deep semantic representation $\mathbf{H}$, while a lightweight gating network reads the \textit{raw, unnormalized} input $\mathbf{X}$ to compute routing probabilities:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Y} = \sum_{k=1}^K \underbrace{\text{Softmax}\!\left(G_\phi(\mathbf{X}_\text{raw})\right)_k}_{\text{raw-signal routing}} \cdot \underbrace{\text{Expert}_k(\mathbf{H}_\text{FM})}_{\text{deep embeddings}}
\label{eq:rrmoa}
\end{equation}
where $G_\phi$ is a small Conv1d + pooling + linear network (${\sim}25$K params) operating on the raw time series, and each Expert$_k$ is an AAS-discovered adapter head. The raw input preserves the spectral and statistical diversity (autocorrelation, trend, kurtosis) that the backbone normalizes away, enabling per-sample routing that reflects the physical characteristics of each time series window.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RR-MoA: Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters}
\label{alg:rrmoa}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Frozen TSFM $f_\theta$, raw input $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L}$, $K$ expert adapters
\STATE $\mathbf{H} \leftarrow f_\theta(\mathbf{X})$ \hfill \COMMENT{Deep embeddings (normalized)}
\STATE $\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \text{Softmax}(G_\phi(\mathbf{X}))$ \hfill \COMMENT{Raw-signal routing weights}
\FOR{$k = 1$ to $K$}
    \STATE $\mathbf{o}_k \leftarrow \text{Expert}_k(\mathbf{H})$ \hfill \COMMENT{Expert processes embeddings}
\ENDFOR
\STATE $\mathbf{Y} \leftarrow \sum_k w_k \cdot \mathbf{o}_k$ \hfill \COMMENT{Weighted combination}
\RETURN $\mathbf{Y}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Backbone.} MOMENT-small ($d_\text{model}{=}512$, 8 transformer blocks). Last 4 blocks unfrozen.

\textbf{Datasets.} ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2~\citep{zhou2021informer}, Weather (21 channels), Electricity (321 channels). These are the standard LTSF benchmarks~\citep{godahewa2021monash, wen2023tssurvey} used by PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}, iTransformer~\citep{liu2024itransformer}, TimesNet~\citep{wu2023timesnet}, DLinear~\citep{zeng2023dlinear}, Autoformer~\citep{wu2021autoformer}, FEDformer~\citep{zhou2022fedformer}, Crossformer~\citep{zhang2023crossformer}, and TimeMixer~\citep{wang2024timemixer}.

\textbf{Training.} Adam optimizer, lr$=10^{-3}$, MSE loss, batch size 32--64. During evolution, each candidate adapter is trained for 3 epochs on the \textit{training} set and its fitness is measured as $-\text{MSE}$ on the \textit{validation} set. After evolution (12 generations), the top-5 adapters are retrained for 15 epochs and evaluated on the held-out \textit{test} set. This three-way split ensures that architecture search (validation) and final evaluation (test) use disjoint data.

\textbf{Fixed adapter baselines.} Three hand-designed adapters representing TSFM practice: \textit{linear} (mean pool + linear, 49K), \textit{attention} (learned attention pool + linear, 50K), \textit{conv} (Conv1d downsample + mean pool + linear, 209K---the strongest). All use identical training setup (same optimizer, lr, epochs, unfrozen blocks); only the adapter architecture differs.

\subsection{Factorial Decomposition}
\label{sec:decomposition}

We decompose the contributions of three factors using 7 datasets on a separate evaluation protocol (see Appendix for details): (1) search space representation (code vs.\ discrete), (2) evolutionary selection pressure, and (3) LLM guidance.

\textbf{Code space $\gg$ discrete space.} All code-space methods outperform discrete evolutionary search (Evo-3epoch, 2,160 configurations) on 7/7 datasets (Wilcoxon signed-rank $p{=}0.031$, Cohen's $d{=}0.82$). The search space representation is the dominant factor.

\textbf{LLM patterns are individually superior.} Within AAS runs, adapters containing LLM-discovered patterns (depthwise convolution, learned feature attention, BatchNorm gating) achieve 19.6\% lower MSE than basic architectures ($p{=}0.003$, Mann-Whitney $U$, $n{=}90$ across 3 seeds, Cohen's $d{=}0.67$). LLM adapters also use 38\% fewer parameters (mean 110K vs.\ 177K).

\textbf{Validity determines aggregate winner.} Despite individual superiority, a static LLM (GPT-4o-mini) loses to random templates on 5/7 datasets in aggregate. The reason: the LLM wastes 25\% of evaluations on invalid code (shape mismatches, parameter overflows), while templates achieve 100\% validity. Templates win on \textit{quantity of valid evaluations}, not quality.

\textbf{Self-improving loop.} Fine-tuning Qwen-2.5-3B on 155 evolutionary discoveries via QLoRA restores 100\% validity while retaining LLM-quality architectural patterns, converging 4$\times$ faster (generation 1 vs.\ generation 5) with 33\% more compact adapters.

\textbf{Search budget analysis.} Figure~\ref{fig:budget} shows test MSE as a function of total evaluation budget for both pure random sampling and evolutionary search on ETTh1 and ETTm1. All 14 configurations beat the conv baseline (dashed lines) on both datasets, confirming that even 20 random evaluations (3 minutes) suffice to discover a competitive adapter. No consistent advantage emerges for evolutionary selection over pure random sampling at the same budget, reinforcing that the search space definition---not the search algorithm---is the dominant contribution.

The budget scaling data permits a formal characterization of why random search suffices:

\begin{proposition}[Search Budget Sufficiency]
\label{prop:budget}
Let $f$ denote the fraction of architectures in $\mathcal{G}_\emph{tmpl}$ that achieve lower test MSE than the best adapter in $\mathcal{C}$. For $B$ independent random samples from $\mathcal{G}_\emph{tmpl}$, the probability that at least one outperforms $\mathcal{C}$ is:
\begin{equation}
P(\emph{AAS success}) = 1 - (1-f)^B
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

From Figure~\ref{fig:budget}, we estimate $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$: of the ${\sim}1{,}200$ architectures in $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$, approximately 40--60 outperform the best discrete adapter. This implies $B \geq 60$ suffices for ${\geq}95\%$ success probability. The proposition explains three observations: (i)~random sampling with $B{=}20$ already achieves ${\sim}64\%$ success; (ii)~$B{=}60$ achieves near-certain success; (iii)~evolutionary selection provides no advantage over random sampling when $B \gg 1/f$, because random coverage already saturates the good region of $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$. This characterization also clarifies the value of expanding the search space: moving from $\mathcal{C}$ to $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ increases $f$ from $0$ (no architecture in $\mathcal{C}$ beats itself) to $0.03{-}0.05$, enabling the consistent improvements we observe.

\subsection{Discovered Architectures}
\label{sec:architectures}

AAS discovers adapter architectures that import cross-domain design patterns absent from any published TSFM adapter:

\textbf{Depthwise separable convolution} (Weather, 158K params). Applies channel-wise temporal smoothing via grouped Conv1d with learned positional weights---a pattern from MobileNet~\citep{howard2017mobilenets} never previously applied to TSFM adapters.

\textbf{Conv1d + BatchNorm} (ETTh1, 118K params). Two-layer convolutional feature extractor with batch normalization~\citep{ioffe2015batchnorm} before pooling---a standard vision CNN pattern~\citep{he2016resnet} related to pre-norm architectures~\citep{xiong2020prenorm} that stabilize training with unfrozen backbone layers. This architecture has not previously been applied to TSFM adapters.

\textbf{Learned feature attention} (ETTm1, 50K params). A learnable weight vector applies soft feature selection via softmax before temporal aggregation, related to channel attention mechanisms~\citep{hu2018senet} but simpler---inverting the standard pool-then-project order used in all existing TSFM adapters.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{The time-series discretization gap.} Standard continuous NAS methods fail systematically on frozen TSFMs. We tested two continuous relaxations: (i)~DARTS~\citep{liu2019darts} supernet with learned architecture weights achieves 1/6 wins after discretization---the soft mixture learns co-adapted operation weights that break when snapped to hard selections; (ii)~mixture-of-experts routing with per-sample adapter selection achieves 1/18 wins (6\%)---the router collapses to a single expert because TSFM encoder hidden states are spectrally homogeneous after LayerNorm, providing no routing signal. Both failure modes are \textit{specific to frozen-backbone TSFMs}: vision transformers retain token-level heterogeneity that sustains routing, while TSFMs normalize it away. This establishes discrete programmatic search as a necessity for this domain, not merely a design choice.

\textbf{Pareto efficiency.} AAS discovers adapters that are simultaneously more accurate \textit{and} more compact than hand-designed baselines. Of 30 evolved adapters across 6 datasets, 19 (63\%) strictly Pareto-dominate the best fixed baseline---achieving lower MSE with fewer parameters (mean 115K vs.\ 209K). This counters the concern that searched architectures might achieve gains through brute parameter scaling.

\textbf{Search space $>$ search algorithm.} Our decomposition reveals that the search space representation matters more than the search operator's intelligence---a finding relevant to the broader FunSearch/PartEvo/SOAR community. We tested four search operators (random sampling, evolutionary selection, block-level crossover, and LLM-guided generation) against the same template grammar $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$. None consistently outperforms random sampling (Proposition~\ref{prop:budget}). This is because the quality fraction $f \approx 0.03{-}0.05$ makes $B{=}60$ random samples sufficient for ${\geq}95\%$ success---the space is rich enough that naive exploration finds consistent improvements. However, the LLM discovers architectural patterns \textit{outside} $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ that are 19.6\% better individually ($p{=}0.003$). These LLM-discovered patterns serve as the diverse expert pool in RR-MoA.

\textbf{Cross-domain architectural transfer.} AAS automatically discovers patterns from other domains---depthwise convolutions from mobile vision~\citep{howard2017mobilenets}, BatchNorm from CNNs~\citep{he2016resnet, ioffe2015batchnorm}, channel attention from SE-Net~\citep{hu2018senet}---mirroring recent findings on cross-modal transfer~\citep{shen2025visionts}. A cross-dataset transfer matrix shows only 35\% off-diagonal transfer, confirming adapters are task-dependent. RR-MoA sidesteps this limitation by learning per-sample routing among diverse experts.

% Grammar Augmentation moved to Appendix

\textbf{Limitations.} (1)~The frozen-backbone MSEs (${\sim}1.0$ on ETTh1) are higher than published full fine-tuning numbers (${\sim}0.37$ for PatchTST~\citep{nie2023patchtst}). Our contribution is relative improvement \textit{within} the frozen-backbone paradigm, not absolute SOTA. (2)~Discovered architectures transfer to only 35\% of other datasets, requiring a separate search per task---though a portfolio approach reduces this to 6 minutes of evaluation. (3)~H=336/720 improvements are marginal (--0.1\% to --0.6\%), suggesting diminishing returns at longer horizons. (4)~Continuous NAS methods (DARTS, mixture-of-experts) fail on frozen TSFMs due to the discretization gap (\S5), limiting the search to discrete programmatic approaches.

\section{Conclusion}

We identified a fundamental routing failure in adapting time series foundation models: backbone normalization destroys the hidden-state heterogeneity that mixture-of-experts routing depends on (1/18 wins). Our solution, \textbf{Raw-Routed Mixture of Adapters (RR-MoA)}, bypasses the backbone for routing decisions by reading the raw input signal, achieving \textbf{17/18 wins with 56\% average MSE improvement}---using the exact same experts that fail under standard routing. To populate the expert pool with diverse topologies, we formalized \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: a code-level search space that strictly subsumes all standard adapters and discovers novel cross-domain patterns (depthwise convolutions, BatchNorm gating, learned feature attention) that Pareto-dominate hand-designed baselines. The combination resolves two bottlenecks: AAS provides \textit{what} experts to use, and raw routing provides \textit{how} to select among them. We hope this work demonstrates that unlocking TSFM adaptation requires rethinking both the adapter architecture and the information flow used to control it.
