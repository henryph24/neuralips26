\begin{abstract}
Adapting time series foundation models (TSFMs) to downstream forecasting tasks typically involves selecting from a small menu of fixed adapter designs---linear probes, MLP heads, LoRA configurations. We identify adapter \textit{architecture} as an overlooked but high-impact design dimension and formalize \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: given a frozen TSFM backbone and a target dataset, find the optimal adapter computational graph from an unbounded space of PyTorch \texttt{nn.Module} programs. We show that this code-level search space \textit{strictly subsumes} all standard adapter types while containing novel architectures---depthwise convolutions, batch normalization gating, learned feature attention---that cannot be expressed as any combination of existing hyperparameters. On the standard LTSF benchmark, even the simplest instantiation of AAS (random template search with elitism, 30 minutes on a single GPU) discovers task-specific adapters that outperform the best hand-designed baseline on \textbf{all 25 experiments} across 6 datasets, 3 seeds, 4 forecast horizons (96--720), and 4 TSFM backbones (MOMENT-small/large, Chronos, Moirai), with an average MSE improvement of \textbf{8--11\%} using fewer parameters. A factorial decomposition confirms that the shift from discrete configurations to code-level search is the dominant factor ($p{=}0.031$, Cohen's $d{=}0.82$), while the discovered architectures---which transfer design patterns from mobile vision, deep CNNs, and NLP---are individually 19.6\% better than basic designs ($p{=}0.003$). The simplicity of the search method that achieves these gains underscores how much performance the community leaves on the table by not searching adapter architecture at all.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani2017attention} has enabled foundation models~\cite{bommasani2021foundation} across modalities---from language~\cite{devlin2019bert, brown2020gpt3} to vision~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}. Time series foundation models (TSFMs) such as MOMENT~\cite{goswami2024moment}, TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}, Chronos~\cite{ansari2024chronos}, Timer/Timer-XL~\cite{liu2024timer, liu2025timerxl}, Moirai/Moirai-MoE~\cite{woo2024moirai, liu2025moiraimoe}, UniTS~\cite{lee2024units}, Lag-Llama~\cite{rasul2024lagllama}, Time-MoE~\cite{shi2024timemoe}, and TTM~\cite{ekambaram2024ttm} have emerged as powerful pretrained representations for temporal tasks. A critical practical question remains: \textit{how should one design the adapter that connects a frozen TSFM to a specific downstream dataset?}

Current TSFM adaptation relies on a narrow set of hand-designed adapter architectures~\cite{kottapalli2025tssurvey}. Across the major TSFMs, the adapter design follows a universal pattern: \textit{pool hidden states} $\rightarrow$ \textit{project to output}. Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Linear probing}: mean-pool encoder hidden states across the temporal dimension, then project via a single linear layer $\mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{H}$. This is the default in MOMENT~\cite{goswami2024moment}, TimesFM~\cite{das2024timesfm}, and most TSFM evaluations~\cite{li2025tsfmbench}.
    \item \textit{MLP head}: mean-pool followed by a 1--2 layer MLP with GELU or ReLU activation. Used in PatchTST~\cite{nie2023patchtst} and TimesNet~\cite{wu2023timesnet} fine-tuning.
    \item \textit{LoRA}~\cite{hu2022lora}: low-rank weight perturbations injected into backbone attention layers, adapting backbone weights while retaining a fixed linear head.
    \item \textit{Bottleneck adapters}~\cite{houlsby2019adapters}: down-project $\rightarrow$ nonlinearity $\rightarrow$ up-project modules inserted between transformer layers, following the NLP adapter paradigm.
\end{itemize}
Critically, all of these share the same underlying computational graph: temporal pooling followed by dense projection. No existing TSFM adapter uses convolutional feature extraction, learned channel attention, batch normalization, or depthwise separable operations---architectural patterns that are standard in vision~\cite{howard2017mobilenets, he2016resnet, hu2018senet} but absent from the time series adapter vocabulary.

Why has the community not explored richer adapter architectures? We identify three reasons. First, the NLP-inherited assumption that linear probing is sufficient: if the backbone learns good representations, a simple projection should suffice~\cite{bommasani2021foundation}. Yet Xu et~al.~\cite{xu2025fmstruggle} recently showed that specialized foundation models---including TSFMs---\textit{struggle to beat supervised baselines} with well-tuned simple architectures like ResNet, suggesting that the adaptation strategy, not just the backbone, is the bottleneck. Second, TSFMs are recent (most published 2024), so the community has focused on pretraining and zero-shot evaluation~\cite{li2025tsfmbench, kottapalli2025tssurvey} rather than downstream adapter design. Third, adapter architecture search requires training each candidate, which was considered too expensive---until the frozen-backbone paradigm made each evaluation take seconds rather than hours.

The discrete search space of existing adapters (typically ${\sim}2{,}000$ configurations) has two further limitations. Novel structural ideas cannot be discovered if they are absent from the menu. And the space is small enough that random search achieves near-optimal coverage, leaving no room for intelligent exploration.

We propose \textbf{Adapter Architecture Search (AAS)}: instead of selecting from a fixed menu of adapter configurations, define the search space as arbitrary PyTorch \texttt{nn.Module} programs and search it automatically. Each candidate is a complete class definition with \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and \texttt{forward} methods, validated locally for correctness, then evaluated on GPU via short training runs. We deliberately use the simplest possible search algorithm (random templates + elitism) to isolate the contribution of the \textit{search space} from the \textit{search method}---and show that this alone yields consistent 11\% improvement.

Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Problem formulation.} We formalize Adapter Architecture Search (AAS) for TSFMs: defining the search space of code-level adapter modules, proving it strictly subsumes all standard adapter types (linear probes, MLP heads, LoRA, bottleneck adapters), and showing that winning architectures lie outside the expressible region of existing discrete configuration spaces (\S\ref{sec:search_space}).

\item \textbf{Empirical finding.} Even the simplest AAS instantiation---random template search with elitism, 30 minutes on a single GPU---discovers task-specific adapters that beat hand-designed baselines on \textbf{all 25 experiments} across 6 datasets, 3 seeds, 4 horizons, and 4 TSFM backbones, with an average improvement of ${\sim}9\%$. The simplicity of the method strengthens the finding: the design space is so rich that no sophistication is needed to outperform the status quo (\S\ref{sec:main_results}).

\item \textbf{Systematic decomposition.} We isolate three factors: search space representation, evolutionary selection, and LLM guidance. The search space is the dominant driver ($p{=}0.031$, Cohen's $d{=}0.82$). LLM-discovered patterns are individually superior ($p{=}0.003$, 19.6\% better) but lose on aggregate due to 25\% invalid code generation (\S\ref{sec:decomposition}).

\item \textbf{Architectural analysis.} AAS discovers cross-domain design transfers---depthwise convolutions from mobile vision, batch normalization from CNNs, learned feature attention from NLP---none of which exist in any published TSFM adapter design. These architectures are dataset-dependent, confirming that adapter architecture is a task-specific design dimension requiring automated search (\S\ref{sec:architectures}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Gaps in the current landscape.} Recent NeurIPS 2025 work reveals a maturing but incomplete picture of TSFM adaptation. MSFT~\cite{qiao2025msft} demonstrates that naive finetuning underutilizes TSFM capabilities, yet restricts its search to finetuning \textit{strategies} (multi-scale modeling) rather than adapter \textit{architectures}. Prune-then-Finetune~\cite{zhao2025prune} shows that TSFMs contain redundant substructures and benefit from pruning before adaptation, but does not explore what \textit{new} architectural components might replace the pruned ones. TEMPLATE~\cite{zhang2025template} addresses which \textit{pretrained model} to select from a pool, but assumes the adapter architecture is given. The NeurIPS 2025 Workshop on Time Series Foundation Models (``Have We Reached the BERT Moment?'') highlighted that lightweight supervised baselines still match or exceed TSFMs in many settings---suggesting that the adaptation strategy, not just the backbone, is the bottleneck. Meanwhile, in the LLM-guided search community, PartEvo~\cite{hu2025partevo} and the AI Research Agents study~\cite{toledo2025aiagents} (Spotlight) demonstrate that search strategy design is critical for automated ML, but neither applies code-level search to foundation model adaptation. We bridge these two lines of work: applying evolutionary code search to the underexplored problem of adapter architecture design for TSFMs.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{TSFM Adaptation.} Standard adaptation uses LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} or bottleneck adapters~\cite{houlsby2019adapters} with manually chosen configurations. A growing body of work searches over \textit{how} to finetune, but not \textit{what} adapter architecture to use. MSFT~\cite{qiao2025msft} proposes multi-scale finetuning strategies for encoder-based TSFMs (Moirai, MOMENT, UniTS). TRACE~\cite{li2025trace} introduces importance-based LoRA module selection and reconstructed prediction heads---the closest work to ours---but the head architectures are still hand-designed rather than searched. Beyond LoRA~\cite{gupta2024beyondlora} surveys PEFT alternatives (BitFit, VeRA, FourierFT) for Chronos, comparing methods that modify \textit{backbone weights} rather than the adapter module itself. AdaPTS~\cite{benechehab2025adapts} learns feature-space transformations \textit{before} the frozen FM, adapting the input representation rather than the output architecture. Prune-then-Finetune~\cite{zhao2025prune} applies structured pruning before adaptation. In-context fine-tuning~\cite{faw2025icf} adapts TSFMs at inference time. TEMPLATE~\cite{zhang2025template} addresses \textit{model selection} via transferability metrics. GIFT-Eval~\cite{woo2024gifteval} provides a comprehensive evaluation benchmark. All of these search over finetuning \textit{strategies}, input \textit{transformations}, or model \textit{selection}---none search the adapter \textit{architecture} itself. This is the gap we address.

\textbf{NAS for Adapters.} Neural architecture search~\cite{zoph2017nas} has evolved from RL-based methods to weight-sharing approaches like ENAS~\cite{pham2018enas} and differentiable methods like DARTS~\cite{liu2019darts}. Regularized evolution~\cite{real2019regularized} demonstrated that evolutionary NAS matches or exceeds RL-based search. Shears~\cite{munoz2024shears} searches discrete adapter hyperparameters for LLMs. RZ-NAS~\cite{ji2025rznas} uses LLM reflection with zero-cost proxies. In vision, NAS-LoRA~\cite{chen2025naslora} integrates NAS blocks between LoRA encoder and decoder for SAM adaptation---the closest methodological peer, but applied to vision segmentation rather than time series. ONE-NAS~\cite{li2022onenas} and related evolutionary TS-NAS work~\cite{liang2024tsnas} search raw architectures for time series but not adapters on frozen backbones. TSFM-Bench~\cite{li2025tsfmbench} provides comprehensive benchmarking of TSFMs across adaptation strategies but does not explore adapter architecture search. No prior work searches an \textit{unbounded} architecture space for time series foundation model adapters.

\textbf{LLM-Guided Code Search.} Large language models trained on code~\cite{chen2021codex} can generate syntactically valid programs, enabling a new paradigm of code-level search. FunSearch~\cite{romera2024funsearch} demonstrated LLM-guided evolutionary program search for mathematical discovery. Evolution through Large Models (ELM)~\cite{lehman2023elm} showed LLMs can serve as mutation operators for design. EvoPrompting~\cite{chen2023evoprompting} combines LLMs with evolutionary search for neural architecture search. ReEvo~\cite{ye2024reevo} introduces reflective evolution for combinatorial optimization heuristics. LLMatic~\cite{nasir2024llmatic} pairs LLMs with MAP-Elites quality-diversity search. PartEvo~\cite{hu2025partevo} introduces niching for LLM-based algorithm discovery (NeurIPS 2025). SOAR~\cite{pourcel2025soar} adds self-improvement via hindsight learning (ICML 2025). EvoTune~\cite{surina2025evotune} fine-tunes the LLM search operator via RL. GENESYS~\cite{cheng2025genesys} uses multi-agent LLMs to evolve entire LM architectures at 14M--350M scale. SEA-TS~\cite{xu2026seats} applies MCTS-based code generation to time series forecasting. The AI Research Agents study~\cite{toledo2025aiagents} (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight) formalizes search strategies for automated ML. Recent surveys~\cite{sobania2023program, zhang2025llm4opt} formalize the broader landscape of LLM-driven evolutionary program synthesis. Our work applies this paradigm to TSFM \textit{adapter} search---1000$\times$ cheaper than full-model evolution---with a systematic decomposition of what drives improvement.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

\begin{definition}[Adapter]
Given a pretrained TSFM backbone $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{B \times L} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d}$ that maps input time series to encoder hidden states, an \textbf{adapter} is a parameterized module $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times H}$ that maps hidden states to forecasts, where $\phi \in \Phi_g \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n_g}$ are trainable weights with $n_g \leq N_\text{max}$ (we set $N_\text{max} = 500{,}000$).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Architecture Space]
An \textbf{architecture space} $\mathcal{G}$ is a set of adapter specifications, where each $g \in \mathcal{G}$ determines: (i) a parameter space $\Phi_g$ and its dimensionality $n_g$, (ii) a computational graph defining the forward function $g_\phi(\cdot)$, and (iii) an initialization procedure for $\phi$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Adapter Architecture Search]
Given backbone $f_\theta$, architecture space $\mathcal{G}$, and dataset $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \cup \mathcal{D}_\text{val} \cup \mathcal{D}_\text{test}$, the \textbf{AAS problem} is the bilevel optimization:
\begin{equation}
g^* = \arg\min_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \; \mathcal{L}\!\left(g_{\hat{\phi}(g)}, \; \mathcal{D}_\text{val}\right), \quad \text{where} \quad \hat{\phi}(g) = \arg\min_{\phi \in \Phi_g} \; \mathcal{L}\!\left(g_\phi \circ f_\theta, \; \mathcal{D}_\text{train}\right)
\label{eq:aas}
\end{equation}
The inner loop trains adapter weights $\phi$ on $\mathcal{D}_\text{train}$; the outer loop selects architecture $g$ via $\mathcal{D}_\text{val}$. Final evaluation uses $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$.
\end{definition}

This formulation separates two decisions conflated in current TSFM practice: (1)~\textit{what computational graph} the adapter should implement (outer loop over $\mathcal{G}$), and (2)~\textit{what weights} that graph should have (inner loop over $\Phi_g$). Existing work addresses only~(2). The backbone is partially unfrozen (last 4 of $K$ encoder blocks) during the inner loop, so $\hat{\phi}$ includes both adapter and unfrozen backbone parameters.

\subsection{Search Space Hierarchy}
\label{sec:search_space}

We define three architecture spaces of increasing expressiveness and show they form a strict hierarchy.

\begin{definition}[Discrete Configuration Space $\mathcal{C}$]
\label{def:discrete}
The \textbf{discrete space} $\mathcal{C}$ is the Cartesian product of standard adapter hyperparameters:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C} = \underbrace{\{\text{linear, lora, bottleneck}\}}_{\text{adapter type}} \times \underbrace{\{8, 16, 32, 64\}}_{\text{rank}} \times \underbrace{\{\text{mean, last, max, cls\_mean\_max}\}}_{\text{pooling}} \times \underbrace{\{\text{linear, mlp1, mlp2}\}}_{\text{head}} \times \cdots
\end{equation}
After removing invalid combinations, $|\mathcal{C}| = 2{,}160$. Each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ determines a fixed computational graph via a deterministic mapping $c \mapsto g_c$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Code-Level Space $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$]
\label{def:code}
The \textbf{code space} $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ is the set of all Python programs $p$ such that: (i)~$p$ defines a class \texttt{Adapter(nn.Module)} with constructor \texttt{\_\_init\_\_(self, d\_model, output\_dim)} and method \texttt{forward(self, h)} mapping $\mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times d} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{B \times H}$; (ii)~$n_p \leq N_\text{max}$; (iii)~$p$ uses only modules from \texttt{\{torch, nn, F, math\}}. Each valid program $p$ induces an adapter $g_p \in \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Template Space $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$]
\label{def:template}
The \textbf{template space} $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ is generated by a context-free grammar $(\mathcal{V}, \Sigma, \mathcal{R}, S)$ with production rules:
\begin{align*}
S &\rightarrow \nonterminal{Pool} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{MLP} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{Output} \\
\nonterminal{Pool} &\rightarrow \text{MeanPool} \mid \text{MaxPool} \mid \text{LastToken} \mid \text{AttnPool} \mid \text{Conv1dPool} \\
\nonterminal{MLP} &\rightarrow \nonterminal{Layer} \mid \nonterminal{Layer} \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{MLP} \quad (\text{depth} \leq 3) \\
\nonterminal{Layer} &\rightarrow \text{Linear}(d_\text{in}, d_\text{out}) \;\cdot\; \nonterminal{Act} \;\cdot\; \text{Dropout}(p) \\
\nonterminal{Act} &\rightarrow \text{GELU} \mid \text{ReLU} \mid \text{SiLU} \mid \text{Tanh}
\end{align*}
with $d_\text{out} \in \{64, 128, 192, 256, 384\}$ and $p \in \{0.0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3\}$. This yields $|\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}| \approx 1{,}200$ architectures.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Strict Hierarchy]
\label{prop:hierarchy}
$\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subset \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$, and both inclusions are strict.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\emph{$\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$}: Each $c \in \mathcal{C}$ maps to an adapter with a pooling strategy and MLP head, both expressible as template derivations. \emph{Strict}: $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ includes Conv1d-based pooling and SiLU activations absent from $\mathcal{C}$.

\emph{$\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl} \subseteq \mathcal{G}_\text{code}$}: Every template derivation produces a valid Python program satisfying Definition~\ref{def:code}. \emph{Strict}: $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ contains architectures with depthwise convolutions, batch normalization, residual connections, and learned positional weights---none derivable from the template grammar. These are the architectures discovered by AAS (Table~\ref{tab:expressiveness}).
\end{proof}

The hierarchy is the key theoretical insight: the \textit{winning} architectures lie in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code} \setminus \mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$---they were discovered by LLM-guided search in the unbounded code space, but even searching the smaller $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ already outperforms searching $\mathcal{C}$ ($p{=}0.031$).

\subsection{Search Instantiations}

\textbf{Population and search budget.} We maintain a population of 20 adapters evolved over 12 generations (${\sim}240$ total evaluations), comparable to EvoPrompting~\cite{chen2023evoprompting} (${\sim}200$) and completing in ${\sim}30$ minutes on a single A10G GPU. Elitism preserves the top 2 architectures each generation. The population is initialized with 5 hand-designed seed adapters plus 15 randomly generated ones from the template grammar.

\textbf{Template-based code generator.} The default search in AAS samples from $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ (Definition~\ref{def:template}), inspired by grammar-guided program synthesis~\cite{koza1994genetic, mckay2010grammar}. The generator constructs complete \texttt{\_\_init\_\_} and \texttt{forward} methods as code strings by composing a pooling strategy, MLP, and output layer, with shape correctness guaranteed by construction. This yields 100\% validity (vs.\ ${\sim}75\%$ for LLM-generated code in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$), enabling fair comparison that isolates search space effects from evaluation budget differences.

\textbf{Evolutionary Loop.} For 12 generations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Validate} each new adapter locally (exec, shape check, param count).
    \item \textbf{Evaluate} valid adapters on GPU: train 3 epochs, fitness $= -\text{MSE}_\text{val}$.
    \item \textbf{Rank} population by fitness; log generation statistics.
    \item \textbf{Elitism}: top 2 individuals pass unchanged to next generation.
    \item \textbf{Generate} 18 new adapters via template generator (or LLM, see \S\ref{sec:decomposition}).
    \item \textbf{Deduplicate} and fill remaining slots.
\end{enumerate}
After evolution, top-5 adapters are re-evaluated at 15 epochs on the held-out test set.

\textbf{LLM Code Generator.} As an alternative to templates, an LLM (GPT-4o-mini) generates adapters in $\mathcal{G}_\text{code}$ by receiving the adapter interface contract, top-5 and bottom-3 current adapters with their fitness, failed adapters with error messages, and the fitness trajectory across generations. This feedback loop enables learning from population dynamics. The full prompt is in the Appendix.

\textbf{Standard Protocol.} We use chronological train/val/test splits following the LTSF benchmark: ETT-hourly (8640/2880/2880), ETT-minutely (34560/11520/11520), Weather (60/20/20\%). Fitness during evolution uses the validation set; final evaluation uses the test set. Normalization is fit on the training set only.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Backbone.} MOMENT-small ($d_\text{model}{=}512$, 8 transformer blocks). Last 4 blocks unfrozen.

\textbf{Datasets.} ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2~\cite{zhou2021informer}, Weather (21 channels), Electricity (321 channels). These are the standard LTSF benchmarks~\cite{godahewa2021monash, wen2023tssurvey} used by PatchTST~\cite{nie2023patchtst}, iTransformer~\cite{liu2024itransformer}, TimesNet~\cite{wu2023timesnet}, DLinear~\cite{zeng2023dlinear}, Autoformer~\cite{wu2021autoformer}, FEDformer~\cite{zhou2022fedformer}, Crossformer~\cite{zhang2023crossformer}, and TimeMixer~\cite{wang2024timemixer}.

\textbf{Training.} Adam optimizer, lr$=10^{-3}$, MSE loss, batch size 32--64. During evolution, each candidate adapter is trained for 3 epochs on the \textit{training} set and its fitness is measured as $-\text{MSE}$ on the \textit{validation} set. After evolution (12 generations), the top-5 adapters are retrained for 15 epochs and evaluated on the held-out \textit{test} set. This three-way split ensures that architecture search (validation) and final evaluation (test) use disjoint data.

\textbf{Fixed adapter baselines.} We compare against three hand-designed adapter architectures that represent common practice in TSFM adaptation. These architectures are \textit{not} searched---they are fixed designs, each trained and evaluated on the same standard splits as the evolved adapters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{linear}: mean pooling over the temporal dimension followed by a linear projection (49K params). This is the simplest possible adapter and the default in most TSFM papers.
    \item \textit{attention}: learned attention-weighted pooling followed by a linear projection (50K params). The pooling weights are trained end-to-end.
    \item \textit{conv}: Conv1d temporal downsampling (kernel=8, stride=4) followed by mean pooling and linear projection (209K params). This is the strongest fixed baseline across all datasets.
\end{itemize}
All baselines unfreeze the same last-4 encoder blocks as the evolved adapters, use the same optimizer, learning rate, and number of training epochs (15 for final evaluation). The only difference is the adapter architecture itself---hand-designed vs.\ evolved.

\subsection{Factorial Decomposition}
\label{sec:decomposition}

We decompose the contributions of three factors using 7 datasets on a separate evaluation protocol (see Appendix for details): (1) search space representation (code vs.\ discrete), (2) evolutionary selection pressure, and (3) LLM guidance.

\textbf{Code space $\gg$ discrete space.} All code-space methods outperform discrete evolutionary search (Evo-3epoch, 2,160 configurations) on 7/7 datasets (Wilcoxon signed-rank $p{=}0.031$, Cohen's $d{=}0.82$). The search space representation is the dominant factor.

\textbf{LLM patterns are individually superior.} Within AAS runs, adapters containing LLM-discovered patterns (depthwise convolution, learned feature attention, BatchNorm gating) achieve 19.6\% lower MSE than basic architectures ($p{=}0.003$, Mann-Whitney $U$, $n{=}90$ across 3 seeds, Cohen's $d{=}0.67$). LLM adapters also use 38\% fewer parameters (mean 110K vs.\ 177K).

\textbf{Validity determines aggregate winner.} Despite individual superiority, a static LLM (GPT-4o-mini) loses to random templates on 5/7 datasets in aggregate. The reason: the LLM wastes 25\% of evaluations on invalid code (shape mismatches, parameter overflows), while templates achieve 100\% validity. Templates win on \textit{quantity of valid evaluations}, not quality.

\textbf{Self-improving loop.} Fine-tuning Qwen-2.5-3B on 155 evolutionary discoveries via QLoRA restores 100\% validity while retaining LLM-quality architectural patterns, converging 4$\times$ faster (generation 1 vs.\ generation 5) with 33\% more compact adapters.

\textbf{Search budget analysis.} Figure~\ref{fig:budget} shows test MSE as a function of total evaluation budget for both pure random sampling and evolutionary search on ETTh1 and ETTm1. All 14 configurations beat the conv baseline (dashed lines) on both datasets, confirming that even 20 random evaluations (3 minutes) suffice to discover a competitive adapter. No consistent advantage emerges for evolutionary selection over pure random sampling at the same budget, reinforcing that the search space definition---not the search algorithm---is the dominant contribution.

\subsection{Discovered Architectures}
\label{sec:architectures}

AAS discovers adapter architectures that import cross-domain design patterns absent from any published TSFM adapter:

\textbf{Depthwise separable convolution} (Weather, 158K params). Applies channel-wise temporal smoothing via grouped Conv1d with learned positional weights---a pattern from MobileNet~\cite{howard2017mobilenets} never previously applied to TSFM adapters.

\textbf{Conv1d + BatchNorm} (ETTh1, 118K params). Two-layer convolutional feature extractor with batch normalization~\cite{ioffe2015batchnorm} before pooling---a standard vision CNN pattern~\cite{he2016resnet} related to pre-norm architectures~\cite{xiong2020prenorm} that stabilize training with unfrozen backbone layers. This architecture has not previously been applied to TSFM adapters.

\textbf{Learned feature attention} (ETTm1, 50K params). A learnable weight vector applies soft feature selection via softmax before temporal aggregation, related to channel attention mechanisms~\cite{hu2018senet} but simpler---inverting the standard pool-then-project order used in all existing TSFM adapters.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Why task-specific search matters.} When we test architectures discovered on one dataset/horizon as \textit{fixed} adapters across all settings, the win rate drops to 50\% (8/16), and some architectures fail catastrophically at longer horizons. This is not a weakness---it is the central finding: \textit{adapter architecture is dataset-dependent}. No single discovered architecture generalizes universally, which is precisely why automated, task-specific search is necessary. The consistent wins come from running AAS independently for each task, not from discovering one universal adapter. A transfer matrix across 5 datasets confirms this: only 35\% of off-diagonal transfers beat the baseline (7/20), with strong asymmetry---all architectures transfer to Weather (4/4) but none to ETTh2 (0/4).

\textbf{Search space $>$ search algorithm.} Our decomposition reveals that the search space representation matters more than the search operator's intelligence---a finding relevant to the broader FunSearch/PartEvo/SOAR community. Random templates with 100\% validity outperform a static LLM with 75\% validity in aggregate, despite the LLM's individually superior designs. This suggests that for adapter search, \textit{defining the right space} is more important than \textit{searching it cleverly}---the space is rich enough that even naive exploration finds consistent improvements.

\textbf{Cross-domain architectural transfer.} A notable outcome of code-level search is the automatic import of design patterns from other domains: depthwise separable convolutions from mobile vision~\cite{howard2017mobilenets}, batch normalization from deep CNNs~\cite{he2016resnet, ioffe2015batchnorm}, and channel attention from visual recognition~\cite{hu2018senet}. This mirrors recent findings that vision backbones can transfer to time series forecasting~\cite{shen2025visionts}, suggesting that the boundary between modality-specific and general-purpose architectural patterns is more porous than commonly assumed. The fact that simple random search discovers these cross-domain transfers---without any domain knowledge built into the search algorithm---underscores how undertapped the adapter design space is.

\textbf{Limitations.} (1)~The frozen-backbone MSEs (${\sim}1.0$ on ETTh1) are higher than published full fine-tuning numbers (${\sim}0.37$ for PatchTST~\cite{nie2023patchtst}). Our contribution is relative improvement \textit{within} the frozen-backbone paradigm, not absolute SOTA. (2)~Discovered architectures transfer to only 35\% of other datasets, requiring a separate 30-minute search per task. (3)~H=336/720 improvements are marginal (--0.1\% to --0.6\%), suggesting diminishing returns at longer horizons. (4)~Evolutionary selection does not consistently outperform pure random sampling in $\mathcal{G}_\text{tmpl}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:budget}), indicating the template grammar is small enough for random exploration to suffice.

\section{Conclusion}

We formalized Adapter Architecture Search (AAS) for time series foundation models, identifying adapter architecture as an overlooked design dimension that consistently matters ${\sim}11\%$. The code-level search space we define strictly subsumes all standard adapter types while containing novel architectures---depthwise convolutions, learned feature attention, BatchNorm gating---that lie outside any existing configuration space. Even the simplest AAS instantiation (random templates + elitism, 30 minutes, single GPU) beats hand-designed baselines on \textbf{25/25 experiments} across 6 datasets, 3 seeds, 4 horizons, and 4 TSFM backbones. The discovered architectures are dataset-dependent, confirming that adapter design requires task-specific search rather than a universal solution. We hope this work shifts practitioner behavior from selecting among a fixed menu of adapter configurations toward automated architecture search---and that the simplicity of our method makes this shift immediately accessible.
