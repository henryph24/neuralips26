### 🛑 Critical Weaknesses & Fatal Flaws (What reviewers will attack)

**1. A Fundamental Misdiagnosis of the "Routing Collapse" (The LayerNorm Error)**
The core theoretical claim of the paper is that standard MoE routing collapses because *"TSFM encoders apply LayerNorm at every transformer block, progressively homogenizing the hidden-state distribution across samples."* **This is mathematically incorrect.**
* `LayerNorm` computes mean and variance along the *feature dimension* (`d_model`) for each token independently. It does **not** normalize across the batch (which would be `BatchNorm`), nor does it normalize across the sequence. Therefore, LayerNorm does not "homogenize representations across samples." Two distinct time-series windows will yield completely distinct token embeddings after LayerNorm.
* **The True Culprit:** The real reason the backbone's hidden states lack "spectral and statistical diversity (autocorrelation, trend, kurtosis)" is because TSFMs explicitly remove this information **before** the transformer! MOMENT applies Reversible Instance Normalization (RevIN), and Chronos uses Mean Scaling as an input preprocessing step. These techniques explicitly normalize every input window to have zero mean and/or unit variance, deliberately destroying the physical scale and trend.
* **Why this matters:** Your proposed solution (RR-MoA) works brilliantly precisely because it bypasses RevIN/Mean Scaling, allowing the router to see the raw trend and amplitude. However, blaming `LayerNorm` for the loss of per-sample heterogeneity represents a fundamental misunderstanding of the TSFM architecture. A NeurIPS reviewer will instantly spot this and reject the paper for theoretical inaccuracy.

**2. The Absolute Performance Gap vs. Trivial Baselines**
You acknowledge that your approach (~0.55 MSE on ETTh1) is worse than full fine-tuning (~0.37 MSE for PatchTST). However, the situation is actually more severe: trivial baselines like `DLinear` (a single linear layer trained from scratch) achieve ~0.38 MSE on ETTh1 with only ~10K parameters. 
* *The Reviewer's Question:* "If a 210K parameter RR-MoA adapter mounted on top of a massive 341M parameter foundation model performs 45% worse than a simple linear layer trained from scratch in seconds, what is the practical utility of this framework?"
* You must explicitly justify the *frozen TSFM paradigm* in the Introduction. Without a compelling, real-world use-case, reviewers will dismiss the method as practically irrelevant.

**3. Overclaiming the Zero-Cost Proxy**
The abstract prominently claims that zero-cost gradient proxies *"amortize the search cost to milliseconds."* However, Section 4.6 explicitly admits that the proxy **fails completely on ETTh1 and ETTm1** (33% to 50% of your tested datasets). Highlighting a feature in the abstract as a core contribution when it fails so frequently in practice is overselling.

**4. Performance Collapse at Long Horizons**
The results show that RR-MoA's improvements virtually disappear at longer horizons ($H=336$ and $H=720$, yielding only -0.1% to -0.6% gains). The paper leaves this entirely unaddressed. 

**5. Missing Modern PEFT Baselines**
The paper compares AAS against hand-designed baselines (Linear, MLP, Conv1d). However, it ignores recent Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods specifically designed for time series, such as MSFT (NeurIPS 2025) or TRACE (2025), which are mentioned in your Related Work but excluded from the experiments.
